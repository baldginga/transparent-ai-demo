The problem

When algorithms decide,
who can you argue with?

The Australian Robodebt scheme illegally raised debts against welfare recipients using automated income averaging — a flawed methodology buried in code, invisible to the people it harmed. The UK Post Office Horizon scandal convicted hundreds of subpostmasters based on accounting software errors that no one could examine. In both cases, citizens faced decisions with no visible reasoning and no meaningful path to challenge them. AI language models offer a fundamentally different approach.

Opaque rule engines
Traditional automated systems apply eligibility rules through code that citizens cannot read. When a decision is wrong, there is no audit trail, no stated reasoning, and no specific point to contest. The logic is inaccessible by design.
AI with a paper trail
A language model working through an eligibility decision produces its reasoning in plain English. Every inference is stated. Every uncertainty is flagged. When a decision is wrong, the specific erroneous step can be identified, quoted, and corrected.
No right of reply
Without a stated reason, appeal is practically impossible. Citizens receive a decision code, not an explanation. They cannot know which facts were considered, which were ignored, or whether the system misunderstood their situation.
Embedded challenge rights
Every AI decision receipt includes plain-English instructions for challenging the outcome — links to the formal review process, free legal help, and escalation paths. A challenge becomes possible because the reasoning is readable.

The mechanism

How transparent AI decisions work

The key difference from both human caseworkers and rule-based automation is not speed or accuracy — it is the production of a legible reasoning record at the moment of decision.

1
Structured input
The applicant provides information about their situation — age, residency, income, assets, employment status. These structured inputs are passed to the AI exactly as entered, without pre-processing that could introduce errors.
2
Step-by-step reasoning
The AI applies each eligibility criterion in sequence — written out in plain English. Where it must infer something (e.g. that savings are liquid), it states the inference explicitly. Uncertainties are flagged, not silently resolved.
3
The decision receipt
A timestamped record is issued: the decision, the estimated payment, the full reasoning trail, and the specific steps to challenge the outcome. The receipt can be printed, shared with a lawyer, or quoted in a formal review.
4
Identifying the error
If a decision is wrong, the reasoning trail tells you exactly where. The applicant can point to the specific line — "you assumed my $3,000 was liquid savings but it is in a term deposit" — rather than arguing against an opaque outcome.
5
Formal challenge
The receipt links directly to Work and Income's Review of Decision process. The reasoning record provides the material for the review — a named claim, a stated inference, and the correction. No special expertise is needed to begin a challenge.
6
Human oversight
AI decisions should trigger human review before they take legal effect. The reasoning trail makes that review far faster — a caseworker can audit the AI's logic in minutes, not hours. The AI assists; it does not replace the decision-maker.
